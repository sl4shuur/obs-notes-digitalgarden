# Fixed-point theorem and Y-combinator

---

## Fixed-Point Theorem

The **Fixed-Point Theorem** states that for any function **$F$** in lambda calculus, there exists a value **$X$** such that applying **$F$** to **$X$** returns **$X$** itself:

$$
\forall F \in \Lambda, \exists X \in \Lambda: X \equiv_{\beta} F X
$$

This means that **$X$ is a fixed point of $F$**, meaning applying **$F$** to **$X$** does not change its value.

### What is a Fixed Point? ü§î

A **fixed point** of a function **$F$** is a value **$X$** that **does not change** when you apply **$F$** to it.  

In simple terms:

$$
F(X) = X
$$

This means **$X$ stays the same**, even after applying the function **$F$**.

---

### Real-World Example üåç

Think of **pressing an elevator button**:

- If you're on the **3rd floor** and press **"3"**, the elevator **doesn't move**.  
- The **3rd floor** is a **fixed point** because pressing "3" **doesn't change your position**.

---

### Another Example: Multiplication

Imagine a function that **doubles** a number:

$$
F(n) = 2n
$$

- If we apply **$F$** to **$0$**:  
  $$ F(0) = 2 \times 0 = 0 $$  

- **$0$ is a fixed point** because doubling it still gives **$0$**.

üöÄ <strong><span style="color: var(--color-aqua);">A fixed point is a value that "stays put" under a function!</span></strong> It will help us to stop the recursion later...

## Constructing a Fixed Point

To construct a **fixed point**, we define a special term **$W$**:

$$
W \equiv \textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{Œªx.F(xx)}
$$

Now, let‚Äôs set:

$$
X \equiv W W
$$

Expanding:

$$
X \equiv \textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{Œªx.F(xx)} W
$$

Now applying **$W$**:

$$
\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{Œªx.F(xx)} W \to_{\beta} F(WW) \equiv F X
$$

Thus, we have:

$$
X \equiv_{\beta} F X
$$

‚úÖ We found a **fixed point** for $F$!

## The Y-Combinator

The **Y-Combinator** is a special lambda function that finds fixed points for **any function $F$**. It is crucial in lambda calculus because it enables **recursion** in a system that lacks named functions.

### Definition of the Y-Combinator

$$
Y \equiv Œªf.(Œªx.f(xx))(Œªx.f(xx))
$$

When applied to **$F$**, we get:

$$
YF \equiv (\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{Œªf}.(Œªx.\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{f}(xx))(Œªx.\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{f}(xx))) F
$$

Expanding:

$$
(\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{Œªf}.(Œªx.\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{f}(xx))(Œªx.\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{f}(xx))) F 

\to_{\beta}

(Œªx.\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{F}(xx)) (Œªx.\textcolor{var(--color-aqua)}{F}(xx))
$$

Now, look at this magic:

$$
\begin{align*}
YF \equiv (Œªx.F(xx))\,\textcolor{var(--color-cyan)}{(Œªx.F(xx))} 
  &\equiv (Œªx.F(xx))\,\textcolor{var(--color-cyan)}{M} \\[8pt]
  &\to_{\beta} F\Big(\textcolor{var(--color-cyan)}{MM}\Big) \\[8pt]
  &\equiv F\Big(\textcolor{var(--color-cyan)}{(Œªx.F(xx))\,(Œªx.F(xx))}\Big) \\[8pt]
  &\equiv F\,(YF)
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, **$YF$** satisfies:

$$
YF \equiv_{\beta} F (YF)
$$

‚úÖ $YF$ is a <strong><span style="color: var(--color-aqua);">fixed point</span></strong> of $F$, meaning $Y$ finds fixed points for any function $F$!

- üîÅ **Enables recursion** in lambda calculus, where self-reference is not inherently possible.
- üèó **Builds loops and functions** that call themselves indefinitely.
